Introduction

Our interface design falls into the category of screens. Our system is a multi-touch screen displaying input from a built in high definition camera. The system is meant to imitate a mirror and would typically be found in a bedroom, dressing room, walk-in closet, or bathroom.

The concept behind the system is that while maintaining the functionality of a mirror, the screen also provides the user with the ability to virtually try on clothes via screen overlays. It also displays other pertinent information to help someone start off their day, like the weather, date, and day of the week. It can also connect to the user's smartphone via WiFi and display the user's calendar appointments for the day, All of these features are aimed to help jump start the user’s day so that their next 24 hours can be utilized efficiently.

Top-level Design

Features

  • Multi-touch 4K display
  • Voice-commands
  • Motion-tracking
  • Facial-recognition
  • Motion sensing & Automatic on/off
  • Integrated Audio
  • Wi-Fi Connectivity
  • Biometric (fingerprint) scanner

Though the selling point of the magic mirror is a touch interactive mirror, some settings should be made physical for easy accessibility that does not crowd the screen. As such on the side of mirror their is a 5 button panel with wifi indicator and fingerprint scanner. It is centered in order to submit to most people’s heights. The order from top down is as follows: sound up button, sound down button, mute button, wifi indicator, finger scanner, settings button, on/off button. The wifi indicator lights up to tell the user if its connected to the internet or not. The fingerprint scanner is to give the user another way to log onto their account other than facial recognition.

The user can use the screen interface with touch interaction to control all actions on screen. Additionally, they can navigate the system through gestures via body-tracking. The base screen is just a “reflection” (created by attached 4k hd camera). On top of this there will be all active widgets. If the placement of widgets has not been customized then the all widgets - except date & time and weather - will be in a quarter circle in the top left corner of the screen, in the form of a rotating pi menu. The Date & Time widget along with the weather widget will be displayed in the top right corner of the screen. The widgets can be clicked on, held down, and dragged. If you click on a widget then it will launch the application, and if you hold down and drag then the widget will move to where you drag and release it, if you hold down on it without dragging then options will appear around it with deactive being a default option for all widgets.

All widgets can be deactivated by clicking the deactivate menu in their options menu or unmarking them in the settings menu. The settings menu can be accessed by the settings widget(which can be deactivated) or the settings button. When you open the settings menu, the screen will fade and a list will pop up. On top of the list will be two adjustment bars, one for sound and the other for screen brightness. The list will include every widget with a square box next to it. Active widgets will have their box checked while deactivated widgets will have their box unmarked. Users can toggle between active and deactive by clicking the widget’s name or the box next to it. To exit the settings menu the user can click anywhere on the screen not covered by the menu or press the settings button.

Usage Scenarios

Outfit of the Day (OOTD)

Expanding on the existing functionality of a mirror, this new enhanced Magic Mirror will act in the same way as one expects of a traditional mirror. The mirror is an essential tool used everyday from the moment a person gets out of bed. It aids us in ways that are not given a second thought. Mirrors indirectly help us clean our teeth, do our hair, and get dressed. Getting dressed can sometimes be a hassle if one cannot decide what to wear. For some people, picking out an outfit sometimes results in a bedroom or closet floor covered in clothes. Although not everyone suffers with this problem, wouldn’t it be nice if there were a tool that could help speed up this process? In the 90’s movie Clueless, the main character, Cher Horowitz uses a computer to pick and choose her outfits. The computer even goes so far as to tell Cher whether a top and bottom yield a matching outfit. Although the latter is something of subjective human reasoning and opinion that computers cannot mimic, the idea is executable in some way. One of the applications central to this mirror is the ability to upload and organize one’s closet, an app called OOTD. The acronym stands for “outfit of the day”. In this application, users will be able to stand in front of the mirror and swipe left or right to navigate through the clothes in their wardrobe. Through the use of motion tracking like seen in the Microsoft Kinect, the clothes will map onto the body of the person, like a virtual dressing room but without the hassle of actually changing. Left and right arrows on the mirror screen will allow the user to physically tap the mirror to “change clothes” and act as visual navigation indicators. Direct manipulation is also being appealed to since this function may also be carried out by physically motioning or waving one’s arms left or right to indicate “next” or “previous”. The user may also directly touch the screen to drag the clothes being “tried on” off of his or her body. Once an outfit is decided on, the user can easily take the items out of his or her closet and get dressed without a tornado of clothes plundering through the room. Another helpful tool provided by OOTD is closet organization, allowing the user to sort and view their clothes based off general categories like dresses, tops, bottoms, and outerwear. The interaction style being utilized here is menu selection. This side menu will contain a list of categories with the respective clothing items sorted into each one.

When the OOTD app is first accessed, the pie menu in the top left corner will fade away and the interface that the user will see is depicted in the left image above. The title “OOTD” will be present only momentarily to provide feedback to the user that this application has started. The side menu is positioned on the right side of the mirror to allow the user to view the clothes stored in each category and “try on” each item by swiping left and right. The default function of this application will allow users to try on clothes preselected by the system based off the day’s weather. For instance, on a hot summer day, the user will be browsing through lightweight tops, shorts, skirts, and dresses in contrast to winter days where the options will be pants, sweaters, and coats. This filter employs the first principle of anticipation in order to increase efficiency when it comes to deciding on the day’s outfit. By limiting the user’s options to what is reasonable to wear based off the weather, anticipating the type of outfit that will be chosen results in higher levels of efficiency and satisfaction. Less navigation will be required of the user and only relevant information is presented so that the program can be readily incorporated into one’s daily routine. In doing so, a user does not waste time scrolling through options that they have already mentally disqualified. For instance, someone would not be considering sun dresses as a possible outfit when it is snowing outside. The simplicity of this application makes this interface so easy to use that it could be utilized outside of the private home. Retailers could employ this interface to allow customers to “try on” on their clothes to get an idea of what something may look like. Many times long dressing room lines or the idea of having to dress and undress results in time and money wasted on unflattering clothes. Of course, the illusion in the Magic Mirror is not as accurate as trying on the physical item but it does allows the customer to envision what it may look like. This would eliminate the hassle of trying on multiple items and save time and money.

Voice Commands

The magic mirror responds to a variety of voice commands. It has been programmed with speech-recognition software comparable to that of Xbox Kinect and Apple's Siri.

The user can speak in natural language to the mirror, using commands to open apps, or to ask the mirror to read information from the user's smartphone out loud. The mirror responds, also in natural language, displaying any relevant information on the screen or displaying it out loud, depending on the user's request. If the mirror responds with audio, flashing lights around the edges of the screen serve as visual feedback to notify the user that there is audio output. The following represents a scenario in which the user wishes to hear their scheduled appointments for the day, and the mirror responds verbally, reading the events from the user's smartphone via a wi-fi connection.

Mirror what appointments do I have today?

It looks like you are supposed to meet Jerry for Lunch at the Cheesecake Factory at 1:00pm.

The following is an example of using voice commands to display information on the screen.

Mirror, please show me my calendar appointments for today

Voice commands can also be used to exit applications. For instance, if the user is in the middle of using the Outfit of The Day Application, all they must do is say "mirror exit", and they will be brought to the top-level of the interface.

Sticky Notes

The mirror’s sticky note interface allows the user to post virtual sticky notes of various sizes to the mirror screen. Upon opening the application, the user is presented with 5 buttons: New Sticky, Delete All, Hide All, Show All. When the user selects the ‘New Sticky’ button the mirror opens the sticky note editor. A big yellow sticky note with a save button in the bottom right corner appears on the screen and the rest of the screen fades out. The user is then presented a qwerty keyboard below the sticky to type in their entry. As text reaches the end of the sticky, the sticky will scroll down creating more room. The user will of course be able to scroll up and down by flicking the sticky note with their hand.

Once they click the save button the big sticky note will disappear and the mirror will fade in. To view the created sticky-notes again, the user must simply click on the application icon. When you click on it, it will bring up the sticky note editor and the text inside of it. When you hold down on any individual sticky note, it will give you the options to edit, delete, or hide it. Thanks to this simple and intuitive system the sticky note interface has strength in learnability and memorability.

Design Rationale

Anticipation

The system uses anticipation throughout to aid in user efficiency. Instead of having to physically turn on the mirror, motion-detection is utilized to anticipate a user entering the room and potentially using the mirror. Thus, the mirror will automatically turn on. Normally, when approaching a mirror, the mental model in the user’s head is that no further action is required. The user expects the mirror to reflect whatever is in front of it. This characteristic is replicated in our mirror to maintain consistency with a traditional mirror. Our mirror automatically turns on via motion sensing when someone enters the room, so that it immediately starts reflecting whatever is in front of it. Otherwise, when no motion is detected, like when someone leaves the room, it will automatically enter a “power-save mode” to conserve energy and the display will turn off. The principle of anticipation is also exhibited in the OOTD application since the system provides recommendations based on what it predicts the user will be most comfortable in on the given day’s weather.

Feedback

Feedback is provided in cases where the internal state must transition to assure the user that the system is responding to user input. As previously noted, the system will provide visual feedback in the form of flashing lights around the edges of the mirror when speaking back to the user. This ensures that feedback is delivered to the user in the case the sound on their mirror has been muted. They will know that the system responded, but they did not hear the response because the sound was off. Additionally, when transitioning between applications, the system will notify the user via on-screen text and auditory responses. When pressing the capacitive buttons on the side of the mirror, they will give tactile feedback in the form of vibrations, indicating that the button press has been received by the system.

Metric Projections

We genuinely believe that our system would perform strongly on all of the usability tests, with no area in specific having any substantial weaknesses. The following is justification for our decision.

Efficiency

We project that our system will perform the most strongly in the category of efficiency. We have designed it to require minimal effort on the part of the user, with each task requiring zero to no more than a few touches or voice commands. Our aforementioned efforts at integration of anticipation specifically minimize the number of touches required. Additionally there are no complex menus to navigate through, as all of the functionality of the mirror can be accessed from the pie menu in the upper right hand corner. Logging in and out of the system is done automatically, with the facial recognition feature, to save the user time. To catch errors that might occur with the facial recognition technology, a thumb print scanner on the side panel provides a secondary alternative to login, rather than having to take the time to input a password as a backup.

Learnability

Because of the simplicty of the system, we predict that it would score very highly on the learnabiity metric. We utiilize a simple navigation model no more than two levels deep. The top layer is the top-level interface. The second level consists of all of the applications, including the settings. Even the options menu is one page, so it does not require the user to memprize complex mental models of the navigational structure of the interface.

Errors

We have provided semantic icons, as well as clearly labeled all of the application icons and capacitative buttons, in order to reduce the number of user errors. The only way we could see the user making errors is if they are not familiar with the traditional icons used in our navigation bar.

Memorability

Because of the efficient nature of the application, and its design oriented towards minimizing errors, we believe the system naturally lends itself to memorability. The user can spend more time focusing on the desired experience of trying on clothes than on using the system or figuring out how to use it.

Satisfaction

Due to performing strongly on all of the previous metrics, we believe usability tests would culminate in a high score on user satisfaction.
